IELTS Reading 3 — The Mystery of Yawning

Passage 1: The Mystery of Yawning

According to the conventional wisdom, we yawn because we are bored or sleepy and need to take in more oxygen. Unfortunately, if you think about it, this theory makes little sense. For a start, if yawning were driven by a need for oxygen, you would expect it to be contagious — and it is. But if yawning is a social behaviour, what exactly is it communicating?

The yawn signal, if that is what it is, appears to be extremely powerful. Not only do we yawn when we see others yawn, but we yawn when we read about yawning, when we think about it, and even, on occasion, when someone describes yawning to us. Such is the power of the yawn that some people will yawn during a conversation about yawning. You are probably yawning now. But why?

One explanation is empathy. Studies have shown that people with stronger abilities to experience empathy are more susceptible to contagious yawning. Autistic children, who typically have difficulties with empathy and social cues, yawn significantly less when watching videos of people yawning than normal children do. Chimpanzees — our closest evolutionary relatives — have also been shown to have contagious yawning, another sign that this behaviour may be rooted in social bonding.

Andrew Gallup of Princeton University has proposed a different theory. He suggests that yawning is a mechanism for cooling the brain. In his experiments, he found that people yawned more often in warm environments than in cool ones. He also found that holding a cool pack to the forehead suppressed yawning while watching yawn videos, while holding a warm pack increased it. His conclusion: yawning is triggered when the brain needs cooling.

Yawning also has important practical applications. Pilots, soldiers, and other people who need to stay alert in potentially monotonous environments are trained to yawn deliberately. Deep breathing increases oxygen levels and triggers the same physiological response as a yawn. Indeed, some athletes train themselves to yawn before competing, as it seems to help them relax and focus.

But the social transmission of yawning remains the most fascinating aspect of the behaviour. It has been suggested that contagious yawning serves as a form of group synchronisation — helping members of a group align their level of alertness with one another. If one person in a group signals that they are tired, the yawn may spread quickly as a means of coordinating sleep behaviour across the group.

Another theory links yawning to stress. When we are in stressful situations — before a competition, an exam, or a difficult meeting — we tend to yawn more. This may be the body's way of increasing alertness and preparing for action. Whatever the reason, it is clear that yawning is far more complex than simply an expression of boredom.
