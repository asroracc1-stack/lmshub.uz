IELTS Reading 6 — The Future of Urban Farming

Passage 1: The Future of Urban Farming

As the world's population continues to grow and agricultural land becomes increasingly scarce, urban farming has emerged as a promising solution to food security challenges. By growing food within cities — on rooftops, in vertical towers, or in repurposed buildings — urban farmers aim to bring food production closer to the people who consume it.

Vertical farming, one of the most innovative forms of urban agriculture, involves growing crops in stacked layers, often inside a controlled environment. These facilities use artificial lighting, climate control systems, and hydroponic or aeroponic methods instead of traditional soil-based growing. The result is that crops can be grown year-round, regardless of weather conditions, using a fraction of the water required by conventional farming.

The economic case for vertical farming is still developing. Construction costs for indoor vertical farms are extremely high, and the energy demands — particularly for lighting — are significant. Critics argue that the environmental cost of this energy consumption may outweigh the benefits of reduced food miles. Proponents, however, counter that as renewable energy sources become cheaper, the carbon footprint of vertical farming will decline sharply.

Urban farming also offers important social benefits. Community gardens and urban allotments have been shown to improve mental health, reduce social isolation, and build community resilience. In cities where green space is limited, these projects provide residents with direct contact with nature and a sense of ownership over local food production.

One of the most well-known examples of urban farming is found in Singapore, where land scarcity has driven innovation. Sky Greens, established in 2012, operates a system of rotating towers in which vegetables are grown in troughs that rotate slowly around aluminium frames. This rotation ensures equal light distribution and reduces energy use. The company sells its produce to local supermarkets at prices competitive with imported vegetables.

Rooftop farming has also gained popularity in cities such as New York, Paris, and Tokyo. Some buildings now feature greenhouses on their roofs that supply restaurants directly below them. The proximity of production to consumption dramatically reduces transport costs and delays, while also ensuring exceptional freshness.

Despite the promise of urban farming, challenges remain. Zoning laws, building regulations, and lack of financial support can prevent projects from scaling up. Additionally, not all crops are suited to urban environments — most vertical farms focus on leafy greens and herbs, since root vegetables and grains are much harder to grow in restricted spaces. Nevertheless, the momentum behind urban farming is growing, and many governments are beginning to view it as a key component of a sustainable food system.
